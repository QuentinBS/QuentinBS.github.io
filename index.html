Featured project

Water Rocket Design & Launch Study

This project will serve as the first major engineering study on the site. It focuses on propulsion basics, flight stability, and how controlled design changes affect launch performance.

Planned engineering study

The experiment will explore how pressure, water volume, fin geometry, mass balance, and launch setup influence flight behavior. It is intended to connect forces, energy transfer, and motion to a real build-and-test process.

  • Variables: water volume, launch pressure, fin shape, fin symmetry, total mass, and center of balance.
  • Measurements: maximum height, flight time, horizontal distance, and visual stability during ascent.
  • Purpose: turn basic rocket concepts into observable results that can be compared and improved over repeated launches.

Learning log

Concepts being developed

These notes are written as active areas of study and will continue to expand with project work and coursework.

Motion

Velocity, acceleration, and displacement

Current focus is on describing movement clearly, separating direction from speed, and recognizing how graphs represent changes over time.

Forces

Force balance and free-body diagrams

Attention is being placed on identifying all major forces on a system and understanding how net force changes motion.

Energy

Stored energy and transfer

Launch systems and moving objects are being examined through how energy is stored, released, and converted during motion.

Data

Graphs, measurement, and uncertainty

Results need to be organized carefully, with attention to scale, consistency, and the limits of measurement accuracy.

Flight

Stability and trajectory

Flight behavior is being approached through balance, drag, fin alignment, and how a vehicle responds after launch.

Method

Repeatability and comparison

Experiments are most useful when variables are controlled, trials are repeated, and changes in results can be traced back to design decisions.
